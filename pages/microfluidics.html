Summary

I am an undergraduate researcher in the UMD MEMS and Microfluidics Lab (MML) run by Dr. Don DeVoe. Over the last year and a half, I have been pioneering a bead-based immunoassay for Ebola detection with a simple trick to improve optical clarity of an otherwise translucent matrix.

Quick and efficient disease diagnostics require precision, innovation, and ingenuity. Current diagnostic tests are often performed by lab technicians and can take 12 to 24 hours to heed results. Accurate and reliable results can influence treatment plans and avoid unnecessary follow up appointments. I am developing a rapid, microfluidic technology that simplifies the diagnostic process. The final product is a tool that can be integrated into a sample preparation device to be used by a variety of audiences including primary care physicians and doctors in developing countries.

The Technology

Rather than waiting for an analyte in a sample to interact with an activated surface, our solution involves an efficient matrix of activated low melting point (LMP) agarose beads. The large surface area results in little to no delays in diffusion time, allowing for rapid binding reactions and the identification of small concentrations of analyte. Quantifying the amount of analyte requires a specific technique to overcome the opaque nature of the capture matrix. Typically, an additional fluid would be introduced to reduce the effects of light scattering and improve clarity. Unfortunately, an index-matching solution must be very viscous to match the optical properties of the matrix material leading to air bubbles, clogging, and increased device complexity. Instead, LMP agarose beads can be melted by an external heater to create a solid and transparent mass. External heaters allow for greater control, speed, and reduced complexity.

Challenges

  • Determine an effective chemical reaction to activate the agarose surface and detect the target analyte

Selecting the Chemical Reaction

Through my research, I found several viable options for activating agarose. I selected a reaction that was stable between 0-100Â°C and could be modified for various end applications (Spencer, Melo, Perez, & Weller, 2005). I then activated 2 mm diameter agarose beads and demonstrated positive and negative controls, both before and after melting. The results were encouraging and supported my assumptions to move forward with the reaction.

Producing Agarose Beads

Using a well-studied co-flow droplet generator to produce the agarose beads, liquid agarose is pumped into an intersection with silicone oil, an immiscible fluid. The shear stress of the intersection allows for continual flow of agarose droplets at a steady rate. Below is a video of one of my generators in action:

Maintaining optimal temperature within the microfluidic chip serves a critical and challenging role in agarose bead production. At room temperature, agarose solidifies into a gel that can alter internal pressures and flow rates or clog the microfluidic chip.

To maintain temperature control, I built Turtle-Bot, a rapidly prototyped platform nicknamed for its 4 acrylic legs. The microfluidic chip is placed on two peltier tiles, one around 40Â°C and the second around 5-10Â°C. A raspberry pi with a PID controller controls each peltier tile based on the thermocouple temperature readings and modulates the fans for the active heat-sinks. While I am currently working on soldering the components onto a protoboard, the cardboard-based prototype was an innovative approach that was sufficient for the meantime and transferable between projects.

To overcome the heating and cooling challenges, I used felt, electrical tape, tin foil, and heat tape to create a reusable and insulated material that maintains a constant temperature of 60Â°C within the syringe (pictured below). The tin foil creates a low friction surface to slide the syringe in and out, while serving as a mount for the heat tape and insulation.

Key Takeaways
  • LMP agarose melts between 70-85Â°C and can be melted inside a microfluidic chip
  • A hydrazide reaction can be used to functionalize agarose for a dual-antibody capture diagnostic
  • Through selective heating and cooling, agarose droplets can be made with a diameter smaller than 30 Âµm
  • Rapid prototyping is useful for customizing microfluidic setups for a quick turnaround
